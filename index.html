The National Football League, or NFL as it'll be referred to going forward, is a confusing mess of a landscape that often leaves the average person confused regarding its inner-workings and various mechanisms. Before getting into the project, it's important to lay a strong groundwork for you to understand what is happening. The NFL as a league is first broken into two conferences: the American Football League (or AFC), and the National Football League (or NFC). Today, the teams within these conferences compete throughout the season (along with some smaller inter-conference play), and after 17 weeks of regular season play, and then 3 more weeks of playoff play, the best team from each conference compete in the NFL's championship game: the Super Bowl.

Going a little deeper, each conference is broken into 4 divisions, represented by the cardinal directions. For example, when referring to a division, one may say that their team plays in the "AFC North" or the "NFC West". Each one of these divisions has 4 teams within, totaling out to 32 teams across the league. Throughout the season, teams within each division play each other twice, once in their home stadium and once at their opponent's, with the team who has that highest overall win-loss ratio (not the highest division win-loss ratio) punching themselves a free ticket to the playoffs that year. Other teams in each division can still make the playoffs, however these 3 extra "Wildcard" spots are given to the teams within each conference that have the highest record. This is to say that unlike other sports, like soccer, where aggregate scoring and point differential matter heavily, football generally does not operate in the same capacity, with a majority of the only having to worry about their amount of wins in each season.

We'll dig into the weeds of more important topics regarding winning and its complications a little later, but first lets set some baselines for our information. https://www.pro-football-reference.com/ is a wonderful resource for all things football. In their catalog, they have data on NFL seasons and players since 1920, back when the NFL wasn't even called the NFL, rather the American Professional Football Association, or APFA. Among data that you can scrape from their football portfolio are overall team records, season by season data, specific game logs, player information and statistics, and even information regarding the draft. It's a wonderful free resource that any individual looking to get into NFL statistics could quickly open up and use to their heart's contents.

For this project, we're going to first start by looking at teams categorized by how much they've won over each of the past five seasons. However, we're not looking for the Super Bowl winners here, nor are we concerned with who made the playoffs - we're going to look at the worst of the bunch, the teams that had a win-loss record of under .250 in a season.

While https://www.pro-football-reference.com/ is fantastic as resource, there are some limitations that cause some of the scraping and data management below to be hectic. We'll cover this as we go, but for the time being, we have to deal with wrangling data from the 2015-2019 seasons as each can only be found on their own webpage. Along with this, the data is only available on a per-conference manner, so before combining the years, we'll also have to combine the conferences together.
